      Unbound Orbits

      As proved in section 3.1, hyperbolic and parabolic trajectories having total energy greater than or equal to 0 are unbound.

      Parabolic trajectory

      A parabola is a conic section with eccentricity equal to 1. The vertex point is the point of closest approach and is closest to the focus. If the distance to the focus from the vertex of the parabola is rp=ar_p = arp​=a, the length of the semi-latus rectum is p=2ap = 2ap=2a. The equation of the parabolic trajectory is

      r=2a1+cos⁡θr = \frac{2a}{1 + \cos \theta}r=1+cosθ2a​

      The angular momentum of such a trajectory is h=2μah = \sqrt{2 \mu a}h=2μa​. The total energy of a parabolic trajectory is zero. Therefore the velocity at any point, at a distance rrr from the focus, is equal to the escape velocity

      v=2μrv = \sqrt{\frac{2\mu}{r}}v=r2μ​​

      The velocity at vertex is

      vp=2μrp=2μav_p = \sqrt{\frac{2\mu}{r_p}} = \sqrt{\frac{2\mu}{a}}vp​=rp​2μ​​=a2μ​​

      Radial trajectories

      A radial parabolic trajectory is a degenerate case of an parabolic trajectory, where the eccentricity eee is still equal to 1, however the angular momentum of the system is zero. The trajectory is a straight line, and the two bodies move away from each other, the velocity tending to zero as they get infinity far away from each other. It is classified as an parabolic trajectory since the total energy of the system is zero.

      A radial hyperbolic trajectory is a degenerate case of an hyperbolic trajectory, where the eccentricity eee is equal to 1 and the angular momentum of the system is zero. The trajectory is a straight line, and the two bodies move away from each other, having a finite velocity at infinity. It is classified as an hyperbolic trajectory since the total energy of the system is positive.
