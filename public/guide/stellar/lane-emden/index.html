      Lane-Emden Equation

      The Lane–Emden equation is a dimensionless equation that describes a Newtonian self-gravitating, spherically symmetric, polytropic fluid. It is named after astrophysicists Jonathan Homer Lane and Robert Emden. The equation reads:

      1ξ2ddξ2(ξ2dθdξ)+θn=0\boxed{ \frac{1}{\xi^2} \frac{d}{d\xi^2} \left( \xi^2 \frac{d \theta}{d \xi} \right) + \theta^n = 0 }ξ21​dξ2d​(ξ2dξdθ​)+θn=0​

      where ξ\xiξ is a dimensionless radius and θ\thetaθ is related to the density, and thus the pressure, by ρ=ρcθn\rho = \rho_c \theta^nρ=ρc​θn, where ρc\rho_cρc​ is the central density. The index nnn is the polytropic index that appears in the polytropic equation of state,

      P=Kρ1+1nP = K \rho^{1 + \frac{1}{n}}P=Kρ1+n1​

      where PPP and ρ\rhoρ are the pressure and density, respectively, and KKK is a constant of proportionality. The standard boundary conditions are θ(0)=1\theta(0) = 1θ(0)=1 and θ′(0)=0\theta'(0) = 0θ′(0)=0. Solutions thus describe the pressure and density profile with radius and are known as polytropes of index nnn. These are commonly used to model stars.

      Derivation

      Consider a self-gravitating, spherically symmetric fluid in hydrostatic equilibrium. Mass is conserved and thus described by the continuity equation

      dmdr=4πr2ρ\frac{dm}{dr} = 4 \pi r^2 \rhodrdm​=4πr2ρ

      where ρ\rhoρ is a function of rrr. The equation of hydrostatic equillibrium is

      1ρdPdr=−Gmr2 \frac{1}{\rho} \frac{dP}{dr} = - \frac{Gm}{r^2} ρ1​drdP​=−r2Gm​

      where mmm is also a function of rrr. Differentiating again gives

      ddr(1ρdPdr)=2Gmr3−Gr2dmdr=−2ρrdPdr−4πGρ \begin{align} \frac{d}{dr} \left( \frac{1}{\rho} \frac{dP}{dr} \right) &= \frac{2Gm}{r^3} - \frac{G}{r^2} \frac{dm}{dr} \nonumber \\&= - \frac{2}{\rho r} \frac{dP}{dr} - 4\pi G \rho \nonumber \end{align} drd​(ρ1​drdP​)​=r32Gm​−r2G​drdm​=−ρr2​drdP​−4πGρ​

      Rearranging gives

      1r2ddr(r2ρdPdr)=−4πGρ \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d}{dr} \left( \frac{r^2}{\rho} \frac{dP}{dr} \right) = -4 \pi G \rho r21​drd​(ρr2​drdP​)=−4πGρ

      If we substitue the polytropic equation of state with P=Kρc1+1nθn+1P = K \rho_c^{1 + \frac{1}{n}} \theta^{n+1}P=Kρc1+n1​​θn+1 and ρ=ρcθn\rho = \rho_c \theta^nρ=ρc​θn, we have

      1r2ddr(r2Kρc1n(n+1)dθdr)=−4πGρcθn \frac{1}{r^2} \frac{d}{dr} \left( r^2 K \rho_c^\frac{1}{n} (n+1) \frac{d\theta}{dr} \right) = -4 \pi G \rho_c \theta^n r21​drd​(r2Kρcn1​​(n+1)drdθ​)=−4πGρc​θn

      Gathering the constants and substituting r=αξr = \alpha \xir=αξ, where

      α2=(n+1)Kρc1n−1/4πG, \alpha^2 = (n+1) K \rho_c^{\frac{1}{n} - 1} / 4\pi G, α2=(n+1)Kρcn1​−1​/4πG,

      we have the Lane-Emden equation

      1ξ2ddξ2(ξ2dθdξ)+θn=0 \frac{1}{\xi^2} \frac{d}{d\xi^2} \left( \xi^2 \frac{d \theta}{d \xi} \right) + \theta^n = 0 ξ21​dξ2d​(ξ2dξdθ​)+θn=0

      The boundary conditions are:

      • θ(0)=1\theta(0) = 1θ(0)=1: The density at the center of the star is ρc\rho_cρc​.
      • θ′(0)=0\theta'(0) = 0θ′(0)=0: The density gradient at the center is zero, avoiding any discontinuity.

      The surface of the fluid is defined by the condition θ(ξR)=0\theta(\xi_R) = 0θ(ξR​)=0, where ξR\xi_RξR​ is the radius of the fluid. Only solutions with n<5n < 5n<5 have a surface. All polytropes with n≥5n \geq 5n≥5 have infinite radii.

      Solutions

      n=0n = 0n=0

      The Lane-Emden equation becomes 1ξ2ddξ2(ξ2dθdξ)=0\frac{1}{\xi^2} \frac{d}{d\xi^2} \left( \xi^2 \frac{d \theta}{d \xi} \right) = 0ξ21​dξ2d​(ξ2dξdθ​)=0. Integrating, we find that the solution is

      θ(ξ)=1−ξ26 \theta(\xi) = 1 - \frac{\xi^2}{6} θ(ξ)=1−6ξ2​

      This gives the surface to be at ξR=6\xi_R = \sqrt{6}ξR​=6​. This solution corresponds to an incompressible fluid star, which has the same density everywhere.

      n=1n = 1n=1

      The solution for n=1n = 1n=1 is

      θ(ξ)=sin⁡(ξ)ξ \theta(\xi) = \frac{\sin(\xi)}{\xi} θ(ξ)=ξsin(ξ)​

      This extends to infinity, hence has infinite radius, unless truncated artificially. We truncate the star at the first root, where ξR=π\xi_R = \piξR​=π.

      n=5n = 5n=5

      The solution for n=5n = 5n=5 is

      θ(ξ)=(1+ξ23)−1/2 \theta(\xi) = \left( 1 + \frac{\xi^2}{3} \right)^{-1/2} θ(ξ)=(1+3ξ2​)−1/2

      This solution has no surface, as it extends to infinity.

      n=1.5, 3n = 1.5,\, 3n=1.5,3

      The solution corresponding to n=1.5n = 1.5n=1.5 is of an adiabatic star supported by pressure of non relativistic gas, or a white dwarf.

      The solution corresponding to n=3n = 3n=3 is of an adiabatic star supported by pressure of ultra-relativistic gas, or a neutron star.
